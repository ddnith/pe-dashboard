The Fama-French five-factor model shows that long-run returns are driven by exposure to market risk, size (small vs large), value (cheap vs expensive), profitability, and investment conservatism. Methodology & sources

Plain-English explanation of every signal, how the composite score works, and why the Fama-French framework matters.

The composite score

Each signal scores 2 pts (green = attractive by historical standards), 1 pt (amber = neutral), or 0 pts (red = expensive). Score = (total ÷ max possible) × 10. A score of 7–10 means most signals say the market looks attractive at current prices. A score of 0–3 means most signals say it is expensive. This is not a buy/sell trigger — markets can stay expensive for years.

Signal definitions

  • P/E ratio (TTM): Price ÷ trailing 12-month earnings. Simple and widely used, but distorted by one-off earnings events. All three indices have full coverage.
  • Shiller CAPE: Price ÷ average of 10 years of inflation-adjusted earnings. Much more stable than TTM P/E. S&P 500 median: 16×. Nifty 50 estimated from siblisresearch.com and IIM Ahmedabad research (~22× historical avg). VWRA estimated from country-weighted CAPE data.
  • P/B ratio: Price ÷ book value (net assets). Connects to the Fama-French value factor — lower P/B = higher value exposure = historically higher expected return. VWRA from Vanguard factsheet (3.4×, Jan 2026). Nifty from NSE India (3.14×).
  • Dividend yield: Annual dividends ÷ price. Higher yield = cheaper market. VWRA from Vanguard factsheet (1.6%, Jan 2026). Nifty from NSE India (1.35%). Not applicable for CSPX analysis as S&P 500 yield is structurally low.
  • Earnings yield vs bond yield (ERP): Earnings yield (1÷P/E) minus 10Y government bond yield. Positive = equities offer more than bonds. US ERP currently ~−0.81% — negative for the first time since 2007. India ERP ~−1.82%. VWRA ERP approximate (multi-currency).
  • Buffett Indicator (CSPX only): Total US stock market cap ÷ GDP. Buffett called it "probably the best single measure of where valuations stand." Currently ~210% — about 2.4 standard deviations above historical trend.
  • Return on Equity — ROE (VWRA): Measures profitability of underlying companies. Connects to Fama-French profitability factor (RMW). VWRA ROE: 18.7% as of Jan 2026 (Vanguard factsheet). High ROE = quality signal.

10-year expected return estimate

Derived from the Earnings Yield method validated by decades of academic research:

  • Base return = 1 ÷ CAPE (or 1 ÷ P/E if CAPE unavailable) × 100%
  • + Long-run earnings growth (US: ~2% real; India: ~5–6% real; World: ~2.5% real)
  • − Valuation drag (if current CAPE far above median, mean reversion reduces returns)

This produces a real (inflation-adjusted) annualised return range. Add expected inflation (~2–3% US, ~4–5% India) for nominal estimate. This is directional guidance, not a prediction. Actual returns depend on future earnings, valuations, and macroeconomics.

Fama-French factor model

The 1993 Fama-French three-factor model (extended to five factors in 2015) showed that ~90–95% of the difference in returns between diversified portfolios is explained by exposure to five systematic risk factors: market, size (small vs large), value (cheap vs expensive), profitability, and investment conservatism.

For long-term investors, this means: cheaper markets (lower P/E, P/B) have higher expected future returns, not lower. VWRA's ~30% discount to S&P 500 P/E and Nifty's lower P/B both represent stronger value factor exposure — and therefore, by Fama-French logic, higher expected long-run returns.

The caveat: higher expected return comes with higher perceived risk and uncertainty. Markets price in this risk, which is why cheap markets stay cheap.
