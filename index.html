Investment approaches — which philosophy fits you?
There is no universally correct approach. The best one is the one you will actually stick to through a 40% market crash. Emotional discipline over decades beats optimised allocation by a few percent.
1 · Pure passive — set allocation, never time the market
Pick an equity/bond split based on your age and risk tolerance (e.g. 80/20). Invest the same amount every month regardless of signals. Rebalance once a year — sell what went up, buy what went down. Never look at CAPE or ERP. This is Jack Bogle's actual recommendation and the most evidence-backed approach for most people.
✓ Most academically supported · Lowest time cost · Zero behavioural risk · Bogle's own recommendation
✗ No framework for lump-sum decisions · Hard to hold emotionally through crashes · Ignores the rate environment
2 · Valuation-aware tilting Recommended starting point
Same as pure passive, but you shift your equity allocation band based on CAPE. When CAPE is above 30 and ERP is negative (like today), you hold 60–65% equity instead of 80%. When CAPE drops below 20, you shift to 85–90%. You still invest every month — you just tilt the mix. Review once a year.
✓ Grounded in Shiller's research · Balances discipline with context · Supported by GMO, Research Affiliates, Vanguard's own studies
✗ US CAPE was above 25 from 2014–2024 and still returned 14%/yr — early tilters missed gains · Requires annual conviction to act against the market mood
3 · Rule-based glidepath — let a formula decide
A mechanical formula replaces judgment. Example: Equity % = 120 − (CAPE × 1.5). At CAPE 37.5, that gives 63.75% equity. At CAPE 20, that gives 90% equity. You update your allocation when the formula says to — not when the market scares you.
✓ Removes emotion entirely · Simple to explain · Backtests reasonably well · Unemotional by design
✗ The "right" formula is unknowable in advance · Can leave you permanently underweight equities during sustained expensive periods
4 · Core + satellite — passive core, valuation-aware satellite
Keep 80% of your portfolio in pure passive — never touch it, never look at signals. Use 20% as a satellite where you act on valuations. If you're wrong with the satellite, the core 80% limits the damage. Good for people learning about valuation without betting their whole portfolio on it.
✓ Limits downside of being wrong · Lets you develop intuition gradually · Core remains fully disciplined
✗ More complex to manage mentally · Satellite may underperform enough to be demoralising · Risk of satellite growing in attention at cost of core

What should I hold instead?

When most signals are red, the question is not "should I stop investing?" — it is "what do I hold with the portion I'd normally put into equities?" Options below are ranked by relevance to today's signal readings.

Alternative assets — ranked by today's signals
What not to do: Don't go 100% cash waiting for a crash. Don't stop monthly DCA investing. Investors who stay invested through expensive periods still outperform those who try to time exits. The research on this is consistent across decades and markets.
Singapore-specific options

Singapore Savings Bonds (SSBs): Government-guaranteed, no capital loss, redeemable monthly after purchase. Apply via DBS/POSB/OCBC/UOB. Maximum $200,000 per person. Current 10Y average yield: ~3.0–3.3% p.a.

T-bills (6-month): Currently yielding ~3.5–3.8% p.a. Fully safe, very liquid. Apply via SGX or any major bank. No cap.

ABF Singapore Bond ETF (A35): Listed on SGX. Tracks a basket of Singapore and Asian government bonds. More liquid than SSBs, with slightly more duration risk.

Syfe Cash+ / MoneyOwl WiseSaver: Money market funds offering 3–4% p.a. with daily liquidity. Good for emergency fund and short-term parking.

Emergency fund first: Before adjusting equity/bond allocation, ensure you have 6–12 months of expenses in accessible savings — especially if income stability is uncertain. This is separate from your investment portfolio.

Earnings yield minus 10Y government bond yield — positive = equities attractive vs bonds

Negative ERP means the risk-free bond rate exceeds the equity earnings yield. Currently: S&P 500 ERP ≈ −0.81%, Nifty ERP ≈ −1.82%. This has historically coincided with lower subsequent equity returns.
