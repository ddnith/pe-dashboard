Methodology & sources

Plain-English explanations of every signal, how scores are calculated, and where data comes from.

The composite score

Each signal is rated green (2 pts), amber (1 pt), or red (0 pts) based on where the current reading sits relative to its historical distribution. The composite score = (total points ÷ max possible) × 10, rounded to one decimal. A score of 7–10 suggests the market looks relatively attractive on most signals. A score of 0–3 suggests most signals are flashing expensive or overvalued. This is not a buy/sell signal — it is a structured way to think about multiple signals at once.

Signal definitions

  • P/E ratio (TTM): Price divided by trailing 12-month earnings. Simple and widely used, but distorted by one-off earnings shocks. Zones set at historical ±1SD from the long-run mean.
  • Shiller CAPE: Price divided by the average of 10 years of inflation-adjusted earnings. Much more stable than TTM P/E — smooths out recessions and boom years. Historical median ≈ 16×. Above 30× has historically been expensive; current level of ~37.5× has only been exceeded once (dot-com 2000 peak: 44×).
  • P/B ratio (Nifty): Price divided by book value (net assets). Below 2.5× historically signals undervaluation; above 4.5× signals overvaluation. Current: 3.14×.
  • Dividend yield (Nifty): Annual dividends divided by index price. Above 1.5% = attractively valued; below 1.0% = expensive. Current: 1.35%.
  • Earnings yield vs bond yield (ERP): Earnings yield (1÷P/E) minus 10Y government bond yield. Positive = equities offer more than bonds. Negative = bonds are competitive with equities. Historical US average ERP ≈ +2 to +3%.
  • Buffett Indicator (S&P 500): Total US stock market capitalisation ÷ GDP. Buffett called it "probably the best single measure of where valuations stand at any given moment." Above 120% = overvalued; current ≈ 210%, ~2.4 standard deviations above the historical trend.

Important caveats

  • No single signal — or combination of signals — reliably predicts short-term returns. Markets can remain expensive for years.
  • High P/E is more justifiable when interest rates are near zero. Always read valuation signals alongside the interest rate environment.
  • The Buffett Indicator has an upward structural drift as US companies earn more overseas — the "historically normal" level has itself risen over time.
  • Nifty pre-2021 data is on standalone earnings basis; post-2021 is consolidated. Do not compare directly across this boundary.
  • VWRA historical P/E data pre-2015 is estimated from FTSE Russell publications and is less reliable than S&P 500 data.
